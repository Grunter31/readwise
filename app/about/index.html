The misunderstood problem

Most children are being taught to read words. Almost none are being taught to understand them.

This is the distinction that changes everything — and the one that almost every school, parent, and reading app gets wrong.

For two decades, Australian education fought the "reading wars" — a debate between phonics and whole-language approaches. That debate was about decoding: how children learn to recognise and say words.

Phonics won, rightly. Systematic phonics instruction is clearly more effective at teaching children to decode text.

But here is what got lost: decoding is not reading. Decoding is the prerequisite for reading. Reading — genuine reading — is the act of comprehending what those decoded words actually mean.

A child can be a perfect decoder and a poor reader at the same time. They say every word correctly. They cannot tell you what the paragraph was about. This describes millions of Australian children — children whose parents believe they can read because they can say the words.

Schools test comprehension. Schools do not have the time to teach it individually. No app has built a serious diagnostic tool for it. Until ReadWise.

Bloom's 2 Sigma Problem — and why AI finally solves it

In 1984, educational psychologist Benjamin Bloom published one of the most important findings in education history: the average student with a personal tutor performed two standard deviations better than classroom-taught peers.

That means the average tutored student outperformed 98% of classroom students. Bloom called it "an educational contribution of the greatest magnitude" — if we could scale it.

The problem was always cost. One-to-one tutoring was impossible to give every child. So for 40 years, the finding sat as an open challenge.

A 2024 randomised study found that an AI tutor produced learning gains more than twice as large as traditional classroom instruction — in less time. The 2 sigma problem, after 40 years, is finally being solved. ReadWise is built on exactly this science.
