The misunderstood problem

Most apps test whether children got the answer. None of them test how they got there.

This is the distinction that changes everything — and the one nearly every maths app has wrong.

For two decades, Australian maths education has obsessed over computation. Times tables. Vertical addition. Long division. The mechanical skills.

Computation matters. It's the foundation. But it's not maths.

Maths is reasoning. It's noticing a pattern. It's choosing the right operation when the problem doesn't tell you which one. It's working out that if a shirt was reduced 30% to $42, the original price wasn't $54.60 — and being able to explain why.

A child can be a perfect calculator and a poor mathematical thinker at the same time. They get every arithmetic question right and freeze the moment a problem becomes multi-step or word-based. This describes millions of Australian children — children whose parents believe they're doing well because the worksheets come back ticked.

Schools test reasoning. Schools rarely have time to teach it individually. No app has built a serious diagnostic for it. Until Burrow.

Bloom's 2 Sigma Problem — and why AI finally solves it

In 1984, educational psychologist Benjamin Bloom published one of the most important findings in education history: the average student with a personal tutor performed two standard deviations better than classroom-taught peers.

That means the average tutored student outperformed 98% of classroom students. Bloom called it "an educational contribution of the greatest magnitude" — if we could scale it.

The problem was always cost. One-to-one tutoring costs $60–$100 per hour. Out of reach for most families. So for forty years, the finding sat as an open challenge.

A 2024 randomised controlled study found that AI tutoring produced learning gains more than twice as large as traditional classroom instruction — in less time. Bloom's challenge, after four decades, is finally being solved at scale. Burrow is built on exactly this science.
